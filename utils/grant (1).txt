    "Project Description": "Introduction and Motivation\nQuantum-gravity effective field theories (EFTs) with quadratic curvature terms provide a perturbative window into Planck-scale physics. When Lorentz symmetry is spontaneously broken by vector \u201cbumblebee\u201d fields, the theory acquires novel propagating modes and modified Ward identities. While bulk renormalization-group (RG) flows have been studied, boundary contributions remain unexplored, leaving unanswered how boundary critical exponents control the UV fate of Lorentz-violating quadratic gravity. Computing these exponents requires quantum-loop evaluations on non-trivial bumblebee backgrounds with general boundary conditions\u2014an algorithmic challenge this project addresses.\n\nIntellectual Merit\nOur central hypothesis is that boundary terms qualitatively alter the critical-exponent spectrum of Lorentz-violating quadratic gravity, producing new fixed points observable in functional-RG flows. We will test this hypothesis through three specific aims:\n\nAim 1: Develop Monte-Carlo algorithms that sample quantum loops up to 3-loop order on ensembles of bumblebee backgrounds while tracking boundary contributions. Technical innovations include world-line representations adapted to Lorentz-violation and GPU-accelerated loop integrators.\n\nAim 2: Extract boundary critical exponents by fitting scaling laws for two-point and curvature-squared correlators. We will implement bootstrap error analysis and cross-validate against lattice-inspired regulators to ensure robust exponent determination.\n\nAim 3: Compare functional-RG flows with and without boundary terms to quantify how boundary critical exponents modify the UV phase structure. We will release an open-source functional-RG toolkit integrating xAct tensor algebra and efficient flow solvers.\n\nThe expected contributions include (i) the first determination of boundary critical exponents in bumblebee quadratic gravity, (ii) publicly available tensor-algebra and RG codes, and (iii) a benchmark dataset of RG flows and field ensembles for reproducible follow-up studies.\n\nBroader Impacts\nThe project advances CISE goals by creating reusable computational infrastructure for tensor-intensive physics. Open-source toolkits lower barriers for interdisciplinary research on symmetry violation, critical phenomena, and quantum gravity. Graduate students gain training in high-performance computing, symbolic-numeric algorithms, and open-science practices. Dissemination includes workshops, GitHub repositories with CI regression tests, and undergraduate modules illustrating how computational methods probe fundamental physics.\n\nTechnical Approach\nAlgorithmic Pipeline\nWe generate ensembles of bumblebee-field backgrounds with controlled Lorentz-violation strength \u03bb. Each configuration satisfies \u2207\u03bcb\u03bd = \u03bb(u\u03bcb\u03bd \u2212 u\u03bdb\u03bc) with boundary conditions specified by a free parameter \u03b7. On these backgrounds we perform Monte-Carlo sampling of quantum loops using world-line representations accelerated on GPUs. Boundary contributions are tracked by splitting loop segments into bulk and boundary integrals, implemented in xAct. Critical exponents are extracted via finite-size scaling of correlators \u27e8b(x)b(y)\u27e9 and \u27e8R2(x)R2(y)\u27e9. Functional-RG flows are computed using a Wilsonian cutoff that preserves boundary terms; we solve the Wetterich equation d\u0393k/dk = \u00bdStr[(\u0393k(2)+Rk)\u22121 dRk/dk] with boundary-aware regulators. Bootstrap error bars are obtained by resampling loop ensembles and regulator choices.\n\nEvaluation Metrics\nSuccess criteria are (i) critical-exponent accuracy \u00b10.01, (ii) RG-flow convergence rate \u226410\u22125 per iteration, (iii) boundary-term sensitivity index \u22650.3, and (iv) loop-order consistency between 2-loop and 3-loop results within 2\u03c3. We compare against baselines: Einstein-Hilbert-Lovelock gravity and quadratic gravity without bumblebee fields.\n\nTimeline\nYear 1: Implement GPU loop sampler and generate baseline ensembles. Year 2: Integrate boundary tracking and extract preliminary exponents. Year 3: Functional-RG coupling and public release of toolkits/datasets.",
